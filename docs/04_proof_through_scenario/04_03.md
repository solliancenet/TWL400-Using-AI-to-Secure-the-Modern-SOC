# Task 03 - Connect failures to business impact

## Introduction

A failure-point table is a security artifact. Vikram (the CISO) and Michelle (the CAIO) don't fund security artifacts — they fund outcomes. This task translates the technical failures from Task 04.02 into the language of business impact: why a well-staffed SOC still missed a full compromise, what it cost in time and exposure, and what changes when an AI-driven response runs the same chain. This is the argument that carries into Modules 4 and 5.

## Description

You will connect the failure points to Zava's business reality — the 50–75 fragmented tools, chronic alert fatigue, the ~25 decoy incidents the chain hid among, and the ~75-minute dwell to high-value AI assets — and articulate what an AI-driven SOC changes, framed for the CISO and CAIO.

## Success criteria

- You have connected the detection/response failures to Zava's SOC-overload conditions and to the AI-driven fix _(Validation: **failures tied to SOC-overload impact and the AI-driven fix**)_
- The impact is quantified where the canon allows — tool sprawl (50–75 tools), alert fatigue, and the ~75-minute dwell time to high-value AI assets
- The narrative is framed for Vikram (CISO) and Michelle (CAIO) — risk and operational cost for one, AI-asset exposure and governance for the other

## Key tasks

### 01: Trace each failure to its business cause

Show that these failures are symptoms of the SOC's operating conditions, not bad luck.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section to view the solution</strong></summary>

1. For each failure in Task 04.02, name the underlying business condition that produced it. The pattern is consistent:

   - **Tool sprawl** — the five stages surfaced in five separate consoles because Zava runs **50–75 fragmented security tools**. No human watches all of them at once.
   - **Alert fatigue** — every stage was individually low or informational severity, and the five chain signals were randomly interspersed among **~25 decoy incidents** — routine risky sign-ins, benign DLP hits, commodity malware, failed-logon bursts — so the team was genuinely *drowning in alerts*. Quiet, correct alerts get triaged away.
   - **Manual, single-domain response** — even noticed signals were handled tool-by-tool, far slower than the attacker moved across layers.

2. Tie it to the headline number: roughly **75 minutes** elapsed from the initial compromise to the attacker operating against high-value AI assets — a dwell time the current model cannot compress, because correlation and response are human-paced.

</details>

### 02: State what the AI-driven SOC changes

Contrast the observed outcome with the AI-driven response from Exercise 03.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section to view the solution</strong></summary>

1. Draw the before/after directly from what you did earlier in the lab. The current SOC produced five disconnected alerts and no coordinated action; the AI-driven SOC:

   - **Correlates across layers automatically** — Security Copilot pulled the five signals into one cross-domain narrative in minutes (Exercise 01), including the AI stage the old model dropped.
   - **Closes the AI blind spot** — **Microsoft Defender for Cloud — AI threat protection** (its real Refund Agent alert, queryable via `SecurityAlert` / `ExtendedProperties`), together with the injected Stage-2 event and the planted grounding doc, put the poisoned grounding and Refund Agent probing *into* the same incident as the identity and endpoint signals — instead of leaving that alert uncorrelated and lost among the ~25 decoys.
   - **Responds at machine speed within a human boundary** — the coordinated response from Exercise 03 auto-contained the low-risk actions (revoke sessions, quarantine the document, disable the suspicious service principal) while routing business-impacting steps (isolating production inference) for human approval.

2. Express the change as outcomes, not features: **less dwell time, fewer consoles to watch, and the AI layer finally inside the incident** instead of beside it.

   {: .tip }
   > Resist listing products. Executives buy a shorter path from compromise to containment and a SOC their team can actually run — say it in those terms, then let the product names sit in the appendix.

</details>

### 03: Frame the impact for the personas

Write the two reads your audience will each want.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section to view the solution</strong></summary>

1. Draft a short business-impact narrative aimed at both stakeholders. Have Copilot help you tune the framing:

   ```text
   Based on the five-stage failure analysis in this session, write two short
   paragraphs for a non-technical executive audience. Do not look anything up —
   this is a writing task using only what we have already discussed.

   Paragraph 1 — for the CISO (Vikram): Frame the risk in terms of dwell time
   and SOC operational load. A five-stage attack crossed five separate tools in
   roughly 75 minutes. Each signal was low-severity in isolation. A team managing
   50–75 security tools cannot manually correlate these fast enough. State what
   the AI-driven approach from this session changes.

   Paragraph 2 — for the CAIO (Michelle): Frame the risk in terms of AI asset
   exposure. The attack targeted the Zava Refund Agent — a production AI system —
   by poisoning its data source and probing it with crafted prompts. The danger is
   an AI system acting on attacker-controlled inputs. State what closing the AI
   blind spot changes.

   Plain business English. No product names. No jargon.
   ```

   {: .note }
   > **Sample output — illustrative; wording varies between runs:**
   >
   > **For Vikram (CISO):** A five-stage attack moved across five different security tools in just over an hour, with each alert appearing minor on its own. For a security team juggling 50–75 tools, it's nearly impossible to manually connect these signals quickly enough to stop the attack. An AI-driven approach can automatically link related events across systems, reducing dwell time and easing the operational burden on the team.
   >
   > **For Michelle (CAIO):** The attack specifically targeted a production AI system by poisoning its data source and testing it with crafted inputs, aiming to make the AI act on attacker-controlled information. Closing the AI blind spot means these kinds of threats can be detected and stopped before they impact critical business operations.

2. Make sure each persona's stake is explicit:

   - **Vikram (CISO)** — a real compromise slipped through a well-staffed SOC because of fragmentation and alert fatigue; the AI-driven model cuts dwell time and the number of consoles his analysts must watch.
   - **Michelle (CAIO)** — the attack's objective was a **production AI asset** (the Refund Agent and its grounding source); closing the AI blind spot is what keeps Zava's push to scale AI from expanding its attack surface unchecked.

   {: .note }
   > 📷 **Screenshot to capture:** the two-paragraph business-impact narrative, with the CISO and CAIO framings side by side.

</details>
