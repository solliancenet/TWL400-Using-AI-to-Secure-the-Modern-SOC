# Task 01.04 — Recognize why signal overload hides the chain

## Introduction

You found the chain in about twenty minutes — but you were looking for it, with Copilot correlating across layers for you, and you knew to cut it out of the ~25 decoys. Zava's analysts weren't, and couldn't. This short analytical task names *why* the attack stayed invisible, because that diagnosis is half of the module's executive deliverable.

## Description

You will contrast the unified incident with the raw alert queue and articulate the specific reasons normal triage misses this attack — the finding that seeds the **Proof Through Scenario**.

## Success Criteria

- You can name why the chain is missed under normal triage _(Validation: overload recognized)_
- Your explanation cites both **signal fragmentation** (across products/consoles) and **low individual severity** (alert fatigue)
- You note at least one **cross-layer** reason specific to the AI stage

## Key Tasks

### 01: Contrast the two views

Put the noisy queue next to the correlated chain — literally, in two browser tabs.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

1. **Tab 1 — the raw queue.** In the Defender portal, open **Incidents & alerts > Incidents** (the same queue from Exercise 00). Filter Status = **New + In Progress** and sort by **Severity**. This is what a Zava analyst stares at every shift: ~26 incidents, ~25 of them decoys.

2. **Tab 2 — the correlated chain.** Open the **SOC Lab — cross-layer attack** incident and select the **Attack story** tab (and/or the Copilot narrative you saved in Task 01.02). Same attack, already stitched into one story.

3. With both open, scan Tab 1 and ask honestly: sorted by severity, does anything make the attack stand out from the ~25 decoys? (It's a single **Medium** incident — and several decoys are Medium too.) Then look at Tab 2, where the full chain is obvious. **That gap — invisible in the queue, obvious once correlated — is what you'll diagnose next.**

</details>

### 02: Name the failure conditions

Write the diagnosis.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section for detailed steps</strong></summary>

1. In your notes, capture 2–3 sentences naming why the chain is missed. A strong answer covers:

   - **Fragmentation (the real-world failure)** — in a production SOC these five signals arrive as **separate alerts across five products** (Entra ID Protection, Defender for Office 365, Defender for Cloud — AI threat protection, Defender for Identity, Defender for Endpoint), each in its own console, with no analyst watching all five at once. _(This lab compresses that fragmentation into one correlated Sentinel incident so you can study the chain — but the fragmentation is the failure you're naming.)_
   - **Low severity / alert fatigue** — each stage is individually low or informational, and the incident itself is just one Medium among ~25 look-alike decoys; under Zava's 50–75-tool load — "drowning in alerts" — it gets triaged away.
   - **A cross-layer blind spot** — on its own, the AI-layer stage looks like routine prompt activity; without correlation tying it to the identity, infrastructure, and endpoint signals, the most consequential stage stays an isolated, easily-dismissed signal rather than the center of a chain.

2. Note the business consequence in one line: roughly **75 minutes** elapsed from compromise to the attacker reaching high-value AI assets, with no coordinated response.

   {: .tip }
   > Keep this diagnosis tight and quotable. It becomes the "why detection failed" half of the Proof Through Scenario you assemble in Exercise 04.

</details>

## Summary

You've named exactly why Zava's SOC missed a full compromise: the right signals — in production fragmented across five products, in this lab compressed into a single quiet Medium incident lost among ~25 decoys — each too easy to dismiss, with the pivotal AI stage left disconnected from the rest. With the incident scoped, the entities anchored, and the failure diagnosed, you're ready to reconstruct the full lifecycle — and find every point where detection and response should have fired — in Exercise 02.
