# Task 01 - Open the unified incident in the Defender portal

## Introduction

In Exercise 00 you saw the alert queue the way Zava's analysts see it — the five attack-chain signals buried among ~25 real-looking decoy incidents, all low-severity and randomly interspersed. Defender XDR has already done something a tired analyst might not: it correlated several of those alerts into **one incident**. Opening that incident — and cutting it out of the decoy noise — is where you start turning noise into a story.

## Description

You will locate the multi-stage incident in the Defender portal — separating it from the ~25 decoy incidents around it — review its attack story and the alerts it spans across identity, email/poisoning, AI, infrastructure, and endpoint, and note how unremarkable each individual signal looks.

## Success criteria

- You have opened the incident titled **"SOC Lab — cross-layer attack"** _(illustrative — confirm at build)_
- You have reviewed the alerts it spans across all five stages (identity, email/poisoning, AI, infrastructure, endpoint)
- You can state that each contributing alert is individually low or informational severity — and that the incident sits among ~25 look-alike decoys

## Key tasks

### 01: Find the incident in the queue

Locate the single incident that ties the scattered alerts together.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section to view the solution</strong></summary>

1. In the Defender portal, select **Incidents & alerts > Incidents**.

2. Set the time range to the last 24 hours. The queue holds ~25 decoy incidents alongside the real chain, so don't just take the top row. Find the incident titled **"SOC Lab — cross-layer attack"** and select it to open the incident page.

3. Note the **Severity** (Medium) and the contributing alert, then open the **Assets** tab — you'll see several (a user, the AI resource `refund-agent-prod`, the endpoint `OPS-LT-0427`, and a service principal). That **cross-layer asset spread** — one incident reaching across identity, AI, infrastructure, and endpoint — is what sets the real attack apart from the decoys, which each stay within a single layer. (You'll walk the full attack-story graph in the next task.)

   {: .note }
   > 📷 **Screenshot to capture:** the Incidents queue (with its ~25 decoys) and the multi-stage incident selected, showing its impacted assets spanning identity, AI, infrastructure, and endpoint.

</details>

### 02: Walk the attack story

Read the incident the way it actually unfolded — across layers.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section to view the solution</strong></summary>

1. On the incident page, open the **Attack story** tab and review the incident graph. The single incident ties the whole chain together across layers: you'll see **`mariya.petrova`** (the compromised user) and the **`sp-refund-agent-inference`** service principal (both render as user/account nodes), the **`refund-agent-prod`** AI resource, the **`OPS-LT-0427`** endpoint, and the phishing email (a mail-message node). Toggle **Group similar nodes** off (control at the top of the graph) to expand the grouped *Users* and *Mail messages* into individual nodes so you can see each entity in the chain.

2. Open the **Alerts** tab and select the **SOC Lab — cross-layer attack** alert to open it. This is a single **Microsoft Sentinel scheduled-detection** alert — not a spread of native product alerts — and the cross-layer detail lives inside it:

   - In the alert's right-hand **details flyout**, find **Categories** — the alert is tagged with all five MITRE tactics of the chain: **Initial Access, Credential Access, Execution, Lateral Movement, Persistence**. This is where the multi-tactic span actually shows, even though the incident header counts it as a single category.

   - Scroll down to **Related events → Query results** (5 items). This is the heart of it: each row is one stage of the attack — **1-Identity → 2-Email → 3-AI → 4-Infra → 5-Endpoint** — with its account, source IP, device, sender, and AI resource. Read it top to bottom and you've walked the attack exactly as it unfolded across the layers.

   {: .note }
   > 📷 **Screenshot to capture:** the alert's **Categories** flyout (all five tactics) and the **Query results** table showing the five staged events.

3. Step back at what makes this hard in real life: the five stages arrived as individually low-severity events, scattered in time and buried among the ~25 decoy incidents — no single one screams "compromise." The value is the **correlation** — one rule stitched all five stages into a single cross-layer alert and incident, which is exactly the reasoning a tired analyst rarely assembles by hand from scattered signals.

   {: .note }
   > 📷 **Screenshot to capture:** the Incidents/Alerts queue showing the **SOC Lab — cross-layer attack** alert sitting among the ~25 decoy incidents.

</details>

### 03: Preview the assets and entities

Get a first look at who and what is involved.

<details markdown="block">
<summary><strong>Expand this section to view the solution</strong></summary>

1. You've been looking at the alert — first **return to the incident** (use the **View incident page** link in the alert's banner, or the **Incidents** breadcrumb), then select the **Assets** tab. The incident's assets are grouped by type: **Devices (1)**, **Users (2)**, and **Cloud resources (1)** — four in all, the same entities you saw in the graph.

2. Click through each asset type and observe what's surfaced:

   - **Devices (1):** the endpoint **`OPS-LT-0427`** — a synthetic/injected asset (not a real device), where the suspicious PowerShell ran.
   - **Users (2):** the analyst account **`mariya.petrova`** (the compromised user) and the **`sp-refund-agent-inference`** service principal (which surfaces here as a user).
   - **Cloud resources (1):** the **`refund-agent-prod`** AI resource.

   Don't catalog them exhaustively yet — you'll extract entities methodically in Task 01.03. For now, just note that the assets span identity, endpoint, and cloud/AI.

</details>

Congratulations! You've cut the one real incident out of ~25 decoys and walked its attack story across all five stages — identity, email, AI, infrastructure, and endpoint. You've also seen that every contributing signal is individually unremarkable — the core reason the SOC missed the chain. Next, you'll use Security Copilot to turn this incident into a single, readable cross-domain narrative.
